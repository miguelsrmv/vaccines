What to Expect & When

A typical post-vaccination timeline

0h

Right After the Injection

The injection site may look red and feel sore immediately. Your child may cry from the brief pain of the needle — this is completely normal. Stay for the observation period (usually 15–20 minutes) as requested by the clinic.

2h

First Few Hours

Redness, swelling, and tenderness at the injection site are very common. A mild fever (38–38.5 °C) may begin. Your child may be more irritable or sleepy than usual. Offer extra fluids (breastmilk, formula, or water for older children).

12h

Evening / Night

Fever often peaks in the evening of the vaccination day. Keep your child comfortable — light clothing, cool room. Paracetamol or ibuprofen (see dosage guide below) can relieve fever and discomfort.

24h

Day After

Most symptoms begin to ease. Injection site swelling and redness should be reducing. Fever should be below 38 °C. Your child's appetite and mood should be returning to normal. If a lump remains at the injection site, this is normal and may persist for a few weeks.

48h

48 Hours Later

The vast majority of reactions are over by 48 hours. Your child should be back to their normal self. If symptoms worsen or new ones appear after 48 hours, contact your doctor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common concerns from parents

Yes, a mild fever (up to 38.5 °C / 101.3 °F) is a very common and expected reaction — it's a sign your child's immune system is responding. You can use paracetamol or ibuprofen to manage discomfort. Contact your doctor if the fever exceeds 39.5 °C, lasts more than 48 hours, or your child seems very unwell.
Local reactions (redness, warmth, swelling, tenderness) at the injection site are completely normal and extremely common. They usually peak in the first 24–48 hours then fade. A cool, damp cloth held against the area can help with discomfort. Do not rub or massage the injection site. If the redness spreads significantly beyond the injection area or gets worse after 48 hours, contact your doctor.
Increased crying and irritability are very common in the hours after vaccination. Try:
  • Extra cuddles and skin-to-skin contact
  • Feeding more frequently (breastmilk or formula)
  • Offering appropriate pain relief (paracetamol for infants)
  • Keeping the environment calm and quiet
If your baby cries inconsolably for more than 3 hours, or cries in an unusual, high-pitched way, seek medical advice.
Yes, you can bathe your child normally. Water does not affect the vaccine. A lukewarm bath can actually help if your child has a fever. Just pat the injection site dry gently and avoid rubbing it.
There's no strict medical requirement to keep your child at home after most vaccines, but if they have a fever, are very irritable, or are not feeling well, keeping them home for the day is sensible and considerate. Most children are back to normal within 24–48 hours.
Yes. A small, firm lump (nodule) at the injection site can develop and may persist for several weeks, especially with certain vaccines. This is not dangerous and will resolve on its own. It is the body's normal immune response at the site. Do not try to squeeze or lance it.
Drowsiness and sleeping more than usual is a very common reaction to vaccination — the immune response takes energy. Let your child rest. Make sure they are waking for feeds (for babies) and can be roused. If your child is difficult to wake, unusually limp, or unresponsive, seek emergency medical help immediately.
Current guidance generally recommends not giving paracetamol routinely before vaccination, as this may reduce the immune response. Medication is best given after vaccination if your child has a fever or is in discomfort. Always follow the advice of your child's healthcare provider.

Medication Guide

For fever and discomfort relief — always follow your doctor's specific advice

Paracetamol
Also known as: Ben-u-ron, Panadol, Calpol, Tylenol
Suitable for
All ages incl. newborns (follow specific dose)
Dose
15 mg/kg per dose (use weight-based dosing chart on packaging)
Frequency
Every 4–6 hours as needed
Max per day
4 doses in 24 hours
Forms
Oral suspension (syrup), suppositories, tablets
✓ First-line choice for fever and pain after vaccination. Safe for babies from birth at appropriate doses.
Ibuprofen
Also known as: Brufen, Advil, Nurofen, Espidifen
Suitable for
Children over 3 months & weighing more than 5 kg
Dose
5–10 mg/kg per dose (use weight-based chart)
Frequency
Every 6–8 hours as needed
Max per day
3 doses in 24 hours
Give with
Food or milk (reduces stomach irritation)
⚠️ Do not use if your child has kidney problems, stomach issues, or is dehydrated. Not recommended for infants under 3 months.
🔄 Alternating Paracetamol & Ibuprofen: If one medication alone isn't controlling your child's fever or discomfort, a doctor may advise alternating between the two. Always seek medical advice before doing this, and keep a written log of which medicine was given and when.

Red Flags — Seek Medical Help

Serious reactions are rare, but know the signs

🌡️
Fever above 39.5 °C (103 °F) or fever lasting more than 48 hours
😴
Unusual limpness, difficulty waking, or unresponsiveness
😭
Inconsolable, high-pitched crying lasting more than 3 hours
🫁
Difficulty breathing, wheezing, or shortness of breath
😵
Pallor, blue lips or fingertips (signs of poor circulation)
💉
Redness spreading significantly beyond the injection site
🌸
Hives, widespread rash, or swelling of face/throat (anaphylaxis)
⚡
Seizure or convulsion (call emergency services immediately)

Comfort Tips for the Next 48 Hours

Simple things that make a big difference

🤱

Extra cuddles

Skin-to-skin contact calms babies and can reduce pain perception.

💧

Fluids often

Offer breastmilk, formula, or water more frequently to prevent dehydration from fever.

🧊

Cool the site

A cool, damp cloth on the injection area for 1–2 minutes can relieve local soreness.

👕

Light clothing

Dress your child lightly if they have a fever. Don't over-bundle or use ice packs.

😴

Rest & sleep

Let your child sleep as much as they need. The immune response takes energy.

🍽️

No forcing food

Reduced appetite is normal. Prioritise fluids and let your child eat as they wish.

🎧

Calm environment

Reduce stimulation. Quiet, soft music or white noise can soothe an unsettled baby.

📝

Keep a log

Note temperatures, medication given (dose + time), and any symptoms to share with your doctor.
